What is Conservation of Momentum?

The Law of Conservation of Momentum states that in a closed system, the total momentum before and after an event (such as a collision) remains the same if no external forces act upon the system.

Momentum (p) is defined as the product of an object's mass (m) and velocity (v):

p = m × v

For a system of objects, the total momentum is the sum of individual momentums:

ptotal = p1 + p2 + p3 + ...

What's happening?

In an elastic collision, both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved. The objects bounce off each other, exchanging energy and momentum.

Formula for conservation of momentum:

m₁v₁ + m₂v₂ = m₁v₁' + m₂v₂'

What's happening?

In a completely inelastic collision, the objects stick together after colliding. Momentum is still conserved, but kinetic energy is not.

Formula for conservation of momentum:

m₁v₁ + m₂v₂ = (m₁ + m₂)v'

What's happening?

In two-dimensional collisions, momentum is conserved in both the x and y directions separately.

This means:

m₁v₁ₓ + m₂v₂ₓ = m₁v₁ₓ' + m₂v₂ₓ'
m₁v₁ᵧ + m₂v₂ᵧ = m₁v₁ᵧ' + m₂v₂ᵧ'

What's happening?

In an explosion, a single object breaks apart into multiple fragments. The total momentum before and after remains the same.

If the initial object is at rest, the total momentum is zero, so the sum of all fragment momentums must also equal zero:

ptotal = p₁ + p₂ + p₃ + ... = 0

Total: 0 kg·m/s

What's happening?

Rocket propulsion is a real-world application of conservation of momentum. As a rocket expels exhaust gases in one direction, it experiences a thrust force in the opposite direction.

The rocket equation demonstrates how momentum is conserved:

Mrocket × vrocket + Mexhaust × vexhaust = 0

This is a practical example of Newton's Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.
